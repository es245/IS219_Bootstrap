Humble Beginnings

Back in the day, when Myspace was a thing, I was the self-proclaimed expert of styling my profile. All my friends would ask me to help them pimp their pages as well: adding a music player to thier profile, changing the background to an image instead of a color, and even how to change their top 8 into their top 20. It's been a long time since then, however, I never really lost interest in the way I was able to make my page look. I took those same, admittedly ameteur skills, and I tried to add onto them, leading me on my journey into front-end development and web design.

Obsession or Compulsion?

I was one of those kids that knew what they wanted to do when they grew up early on. I knew I wanted to create, design, build, and bring to life my own creations. Albeit, I could have been a engineer or carpenter and still achieved my goals, but I was preoccupied with what one might consider a mild obsession with code. Ever since my high school web design basics class, I knew that was what I wanted to do, to code. Whether it's browsing around reddit on r/webdesign or r/programmerhumor, drooling and staring in awe at the amazing portfolios on behance, or going on css-tricks or medium.com to find out the latest css hack, I always find a way stay in touch with that world which has become a part of who I am, and I a part of it.

Some of my favorite tools.

These are some tools I like to use for my projects. Aside from some of these being quite standard in the industry, these tools help speed up some otherwise tedious processes and make my projects easier to handle in general. However, not only do I use well-known tools and plugins, I also like to use some lesser-known ones that haven gotten as much attention or recognition as well.

Sass

Sass has easily become one of my favorite tools when working with CSS. Aside from the countless cool features, my workflow is so much faster, and when combined with a live-reload server for development testing, it's truly one of the best tools out there for styling. The most useful feature, I believe, is being able to set variables such as colors or properties to reuse in the stylesheet.

XAMPP

Some people may prefer to stack their files on an actual server to test their projects. However, I've been taught to use XAMPP and can't really shake the habit. Along with XAMPP, I use the very similar MAMP for my Mac and LAMP on my Linux machine. Also, XAMPP and most of the other versions offer PHPMyAdmin, a web-based MySQL tool that adds a graphic user interface for creating, editing, and managing MySQL databases for online use.

Drupal

Some may find it odd that I take on Drupal over the vastly more well-known and admittedly easier to use Wordpress. However, I have always liked a challange, and working on Drupal modules, responsive design, and setting up a basic Drupal site is certainly challenging. Besides, eveyrone always says that Wordpress sites are for people who don't need to know how to code, so where's the fun in that?

Atom.io

There's many text editors out there, but Atom is a fresh entry into the list. I really like the fact that Atom is very well-integrated with Github, showing the amount of insertions and deletions right in the editor and also giving me the ability to commit right from the editor. It's also gaining popularity quick, adapting most of the most popular addons from Sublime or Coda and itself being very customizable in terms of appearance and syntax.

Arch Linux

I am still very new to the whole Linux universe, but I have a new understanding of those who chose it over the more standard operating systems. When it came time to install it on my own machine, I went with Arch Linux. Although it was definetely not the easiest distro to install, it was well worth it. Everything from the bleeding-edge application hosted in the AUR, the extremely lightweight installation, and the beautiful riced out i3 gaps window manager are just some of the things I enjoy about Arch.

Adobe XD

Although it's still in beta, Adobe XD seems like a very promising tool. Already, people have started designing with and making tutorials for the program. Although it's not as feature-rich as Illustrator or Sketch, I think it has a lot of potential. My beta experience so far has gone very well, and I believe that when the app is fully released I'll be using it much more.
